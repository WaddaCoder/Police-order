Police Duties & Powers
Article 4 is the operational spine of the Police Order — it defines what every police officer is legally obligated to do and what powers he carries. Article 3 sets the attitudinal baseline. Article 125 adds the street-level stop-and-search power. All three operate against the constitutional backdrop of Art. 14 (dignity) and Art. 10A (fair trial).

A.118–125 — Police Order 2002
⌂
Public Order Powers
Chapter XIII gives police the powers to manage public space: licensing assemblies and processions, dispersing those that breach licence conditions, prohibiting weapons in declared areas, erecting barriers in emergencies, and searching suspected persons on the street. These are the powers most visible to citizens in ordinary life. Each has a specific rank threshold, trigger condition, and procedural requirement.

A.155–157 — Police Order 2002
⌂
Police Misconduct Penalties
Chapter XVII gives the Police Order its teeth. Three articles create criminal liability for police officers personally: A.155 for general misconduct (up to 3 years), A.156 for torture, unlawful entry, vexatious arrest and seizure (up to 5 years), and A.157 for unnecessary delay in producing an arrested person before a court (up to 1 year). A.156 is the constitutionally critical article — it is the Police Order's penal counterpart to Art. 14 and QSO Art. 38.

A.18 — Police Order 2002
⌂
Investigation Separation
Article 18 is the structural centrepiece of the 2002 reform. It separates the investigative function from the SHO's watch-and-ward authority and insulates the investigation wing within its own hierarchy. The District Police Officer cannot interfere with investigations. Change of investigation requires a formal board. This is the legislative answer to the colonial model where the SHO controlled everything.

A.35–36 · A.44(h)
⌂
FIR Refusal & Oversight
When police refuse to register an FIR, the Police Order provides two distinct escalation routes. The Citizen/Zila Nazim route under A.35 goes through local government. The Commission route under A.44(h) goes through the District Public Safety Commission. A.36 adds a third mandatory obligation: custody deaths, rape, and serious injuries must be reported upward regardless of any complaint. These three provisions are the Police Order's direct answer to unlawful FIR refusal.

Definitions
Article 2 is the vocabulary foundation of the Police Order. The definitions here do not merely label things — they carry procedural consequences. ‘Cognizance’ here has a distinct meaning from its CrPC counterpart. ‘Head of District Police’ determines the chain of command in every subsequent Article.
